Critical Analysis · GEOG 421 — Maps, Society & Power

"Mapping Kenya for Western Eyes" — Critical Essay

Spring 2026 — Midterm Take-Home Essay

For this essay, I analyzed a collection of five Cold War-era maps of Kenya — a large physical reference map and four thematic maps covering ethnic groups, population density, agricultural production, and mining operations. Based on internal evidence (Germany appears divided on an inset world map), I dated the collection to somewhere between 1949 and 1989, almost certainly produced by an American or British publisher for a Western readership. My argument is that this atlas functions as a Cold War economic primer: it uses the language of cartographic objectivity to reduce Kenya's complex social, ethnic, and economic geography into categories that serve Western strategic interests far more than they represent Kenya on its own terms.

I applied frameworks from Harley (1989), Lee (2010), MacArthur (2016), Monmonier (1991), and Nagaraj and Stern (2020) to read the maps against the grain. Harley's concept of cartographic silence was central — the atlas has no political map, no representation of colonial governance structures, and no acknowledgment of the White Highlands, the fertile areas Britain reserved for European settlers by displacing Kikuyu and other Bantu communities. These omissions aren't accidents; they erase the politics of Kenya's landscape and present it as a neutral space of resources and populations. The ethnic map uses colonial-era categories like "Nilo-Hamitic" that modern scholarship has largely abandoned, reproducing exactly the kind of administrative taxonomy MacArthur documents British colonizers using to manage and divide Kenyan populations. The mining map labels resources with chemical symbols but shows no ownership, no extraction terms, no indication of where profits flow — which, following Lee's political economy framework, transforms geographic information into market intelligence for Western industrial and military planners. Even an apparently neutral gesture, like labeling one area "extremely complex tribal and ethnic mixture" rather than actually representing that complexity, is a design choice that keeps the map clean and legible for its intended audience while erasing dozens of distinct communities.

What the exercise showed me is how authoritative-looking documents can embed deeply political choices through what they include, what they exclude, and how they categorize. The maps look objective because they use clean lines, standard symbols, and scientific-style labeling — but that apparent neutrality is itself a rhetorical move. Monmonier calls this the "strategic reduction of complexity," and Nagaraj and Stern show that the same underlying data, presented through different design choices, produces different maps with different real-world consequences. A Cold War-era mining map of Kenya with no social context isn't a neutral document; it's an invitation to extraction. Working through this analysis changed how I read all geographic representation — including the maps I make myself. Every cartographic choice has stakes, and pretending otherwise is itself a political position.

Core Research · GEOG 434 — Climate Science

CMIP6 Climate Model Ensemble Analysis

Spring 2025 — Python · xarray · NetCDF · CMIP6 / ESGF

Full paper available as PDF.   View Full Paper (PDF)

1. Research Question

What I was trying to find out

The central question for this project was how projected changes in surface temperature, precipitation, and soil moisture diverge between SSP1-2.6 and SSP3-7.0 across the 21st century — and what those diverging trajectories imply for long-term climate impacts. My hypothesis going in was that SSP3-7.0 would show substantially larger changes across all three variables, but I was curious whether the divergence would be gradual or whether there was a clearer separation opening up in the second half of the century as cumulative emissions compound.

2. Background

CMIP6 and the SSP framework

CMIP6 (Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 6) is the coordinated multi-model framework that underpins the IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report. Each model in the ensemble simulates the climate system independently using different parameterizations, which means running multiple models over the same scenarios lets you characterize both the projected signal and the uncertainty around it — you get an ensemble mean and a spread that tells you how much the models agree.

The two scenarios I focused on represent very different futures. SSP1-2.6 assumes aggressive emissions mitigation, with global temperatures stabilizing around 1.5–2°C above pre-industrial levels by 2100. SSP3-7.0 assumes fragmented international cooperation and continued high fossil fuel dependence, with warming reaching roughly 3.5–4°C by century's end. Comparing them directly is a way of quantifying what aggressive mitigation actually buys in terms of avoided change across multiple climate variables — not just temperature, but the cascading effects on precipitation patterns and soil moisture that temperature drives.

3. Data & Methods

How I built the analysis

I pulled CMIP6 model output from the Earth System Grid Federation (ESGF) for multiple ensemble members under both SSP scenarios. The three variables I analyzed were tas (near-surface air temperature), pr (precipitation), and soil moisture. All data processing was done in Python — xarray for loading and manipulating the multi-dimensional NetCDF files, NumPy for numerical operations, and Matplotlib for visualization.

The workflow: load the raw files and inspect coordinate systems, apply unit conversions (K→°C for temperature, kg/m²/s→mm/day for precipitation), compute anomalies relative to the 1850–2015 historical baseline, average across ensemble members to get the ensemble mean, and then compare the two scenarios. I characterized ensemble spread using ±1 standard deviation across members, which gives a sense of how much model disagreement exists around any given projected value.

4. Analysis

What the data showed

Temperature

Temperature projections show a clear and widening divergence between scenarios through the 21st century. Under SSP1-2.6, the ensemble mean reaches approximately 1.5–2°C above the 1850–2015 reference period by 2100 before beginning to stabilize as emissions decline. The individual ensemble members cluster relatively tightly around that mean, reflecting reasonable model agreement on the outcome of the mitigation pathway. Under SSP3-7.0, warming continues accelerating through the century with substantially more spread between ensemble members — uncertainty compounds as the forcing grows stronger and the models' different sensitivities matter more.

Precipitation

Precipitation changes under SSP3-7.0 are more pronounced and more regionally differentiated than under SSP1-2.6. The high-emissions scenario produces a stronger intensification of the hydrological cycle: wetter high latitudes, drier subtropics, sharper contrasts between wet and dry regions. Under SSP1-2.6 these patterns are present but muted — the signal is there but the magnitude is meaningfully smaller. This matters because precipitation changes are harder to adapt to than temperature changes alone; infrastructure and agriculture are calibrated to historical precipitation regimes, and shifting those regimes faster creates more pressure on adaptation systems.

Soil Moisture

Soil moisture integrates both the temperature and precipitation signals. Elevated temperatures increase evapotranspiration, which can drive soil drying even in regions where precipitation doesn't decrease — the warming alone is enough to pull more water out of the soil column. Under SSP3-7.0, large parts of the subtropics and Mediterranean-type climates show substantial soil moisture declines with direct implications for agriculture and drought frequency. Under SSP1-2.6, the soil moisture signal is weaker and the regional patterns are less extreme, which maps onto meaningfully better outcomes for dryland farming systems and natural ecosystems that depend on soil water availability.

5. Conclusions

What it adds up to

The main takeaway is that the difference between aggressive mitigation and a high-emissions trajectory isn't marginal — it's the difference between a climate that stabilizes within a range existing infrastructure and ecosystems can adapt to and one that continues changing faster than most adaptation mechanisms can track. The ensemble spread under SSP3-7.0 was notably larger than under SSP1-2.6, which makes physical sense but also has a policy implication: the higher-emissions outcome is both more damaging and less predictable. You get worse outcomes and more uncertainty about how bad they'll be.

There were real limitations I'd want to address with more time: the number of ensemble members varied by model, the analysis stays at coarse global resolution rather than the bias-corrected regional output you'd need for actual planning, and the soil moisture variable had less consistent coverage across models than temperature or precipitation. But the core signal is robust across those limitations.

This was the most technically demanding thing I'd done in Python up to that point. Working with multi-dimensional NetCDF data, handling coordinate systems, building clean visualizations from raw model output — it pushed me to be a more careful programmer and a more precise analyst. And the stakes made it feel real: these aren't abstract numbers, they're projections that should be informing infrastructure investment and emissions policy right now. That connection between the technical work and the actual decision-making is a big part of why I want to keep doing this kind of analysis.

Featured Project — QGIS · Independent

Great Smoky Mountains National Park / Breath of the Wild

I built this map in QGIS to see how far I could push custom cartographic styling — specifically, whether I could make real topographic data feel like it belonged in the visual world of The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild. The source data is USGS 3DEP elevation data for Great Smoky Mountains National Park. I pulled the DEMs, generated custom contour intervals, styled them with a Hyrule-inspired color palette, and added points of interest using custom symbol libraries.

What I found interesting about the project is that it's actually a good test of cartographic fundamentals — the BotW aesthetic has really clear rules about line weight, color hierarchy, and label placement, so matching it forced me to be precise about things that are easy to be sloppy about in default QGIS workflows. The map also reflects something I think is true: aesthetic choices in maps are never just decorative. They shape how the reader emotionally engages with the geography, what they notice first, and what they remember. That's as true for this map as it is for a Cold War atlas of Kenya.

GEOG 311 · ArcGIS Pro

Tornado Intensity vs. Population Density — American Southeast

I made this map for GEOG 311 to explore the relationship between tornado intensity and population density across the American Southeast. The research question was whether modern urbanization might be driving apparent increases in tornado-related damage in the region — as cities expand into historically rural tornado-prone areas, more storms affect more people and more infrastructure, which shows up in the damage record even without any change in the underlying storm climatology. I mapped tornado intensity data at the county level alongside population density, looking for spatial patterns in the overlap.

The Southeast is part of what researchers call "Dixie Alley" — a secondary tornado corridor that gets less attention than the Great Plains but has comparable or higher tornado activity in terms of frequency and fatality rates. Making this map in ArcGIS Pro taught me a lot about classification decisions in choropleth mapping: how you set your breaks, what classification scheme you use (equal interval, natural breaks, quantile), and what color ramp you choose all dramatically change what pattern the viewer reads. It was one of my first substantial GIS deliverables, and it's where I started thinking more carefully about the gap between "I made a map" and "I made a map that accurately communicates what the data shows."
